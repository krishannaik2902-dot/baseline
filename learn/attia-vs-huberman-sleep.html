Attia, Huberman and Walker walk into a bedroom

Journal · 7 July 2026

Three of the most-quoted names in health each have a sleep protocol. The useful trick with any panel of experts is to ignore where they differ and do what they agree on — because agreement across independent people is about as close to signal as this field gets.

Matthew Walker is a sleep scientist who wrote the book most people have heard of. Andrew Huberman is a neuroscientist who turned protocols into a podcast empire. Peter Attia is a doctor who frames everything around living well in your last decade. They don’t coordinate. So when all three land on the same instruction, that instruction has earned your attention.

Where they agree — do these tonight

Keep the schedule regular, weekends included

All three put consistency near the top. Walker folds it into his “QQRT” framing (quantity, quality, regularity, timing). Attia points to the 2024 research showing sleep regularity out-predicts sleep duration for mortality. Huberman’s version is “wake at the same time every day.” This is the single highest-value sleep habit, and it’s free.

Cool the room to around 18°C

Walker names roughly 18°C / 65°F. Huberman explains the mechanism — your core temperature has to drop a degree or two to fall and stay asleep, so a cool room helps and a warm one fights you. Same instruction, same reason.

Get daylight in the morning

Huberman is loudest on this (get outside within 30–60 minutes of waking, no sunglasses), but Walker also stresses natural light for anchoring the clock. Morning light is the lever that makes the evening easier, weeks later.

Move caffeine earlier

Everyone agrees the afternoon coffee is quietly taxing your sleep, because caffeine hangs around for hours. They differ only on how strict to be — see below.

Don’t use alcohol as a sleep aid

Unanimous. Alcohol helps you fall asleep and then fragments the back half of the night, suppressing REM and deep sleep. Huberman’s blunt summary of the wider evidence: for health, zero is better than any, and the costs mount past a couple of drinks a week.

Notice that the disagreements are all at the fiddly, optional end — exactly the end people obsess over. The caffeine cut-off argument is a rounding error next to just having a cut-off. The supplement debate matters far less than the boring behaviours all three put first. Huberman himself orders it explicitly: fix light, then temperature, then caffeine timing, and only then consider supplements. Pills last.

The tonight list

  1. Set one wake time you can hold every day, and get up at it even after a bad night.
  2. Get outside for a few minutes of daylight within an hour of waking.
  3. Turn the bedroom down to around 18°C and make it properly dark.
  4. Draw a caffeine line 8–10 hours before bed.
  5. Don’t rely on a nightcap; it’s borrowing sleep quality against sleep speed.

That’s five free things, agreed on by three people who don’t work together. If you did only those, you’d have most of the sleep protocol that everyone’s selling the accessories for.
