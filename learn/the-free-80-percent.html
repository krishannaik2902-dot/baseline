The free 80% of Blueprint

Journal · 7 July 2026

Bryan Johnson spends somewhere north of two million dollars a year trying not to age. He also says, repeatedly, that about 80% of the results come from things that cost nothing. That second sentence is the interesting one.

Johnson is easy to make fun of, and plenty of people do. The forty-plus daily pills, the annual MRIs, the “speed of ageing” tests, the olive oil with a trademark. In May 2026 he was diagnosed with autoimmune gastritis — an incurable condition — which was a sharp reminder that no amount of spending buys you out of biology. So let’s set aside the expensive, buyable 20% entirely and look only at the part he himself says does most of the work, because that part is free, and most of it is genuinely well-evidenced.

Sleep, treated as non-negotiable

Johnson calls a consistent bedtime “the most important appointment of the day” and describes himself, only half-joking, as a professional sleeper. The specifics he’s converged on cost nothing: in bed and up at the same times every day, a room kept cool (he uses roughly 15–19°C / 60–67°F), no screens for the hour before bed, no caffeine or alcohol within about ten hours of sleep, and the last meal of the day finished well before bed.

None of that is exotic, and none of it is his. It lines up with what sleep scientist Matthew Walker has said for years, and — importantly — with the mortality data. A 2024 analysis of over 60,000 people found that regularity of sleep timing predicted death more strongly than sleep duration did. The free move (go to bed and get up at consistent times) beats the expensive one (optimising your sleep-tracker score) on the thing that actually matters.

Movement, in a shape you already know

Johnson’s exercise week is about six hours: three strength sessions, three cardio sessions, with the cardio split between easy conversational-pace work and a smaller amount of hard effort. Strip the branding off and it’s the mainstream consensus — the same structure Peter Attia lays out in Outlive, the same one Andy Galpin teaches.

And the evidence under it is some of the strongest in the whole field. In a study of 122,000 people, the least-fit group had roughly five times the mortality risk of the fittest — a bigger gap than smoking or diabetes. Any resistance training at all is associated with about 15% lower all-cause mortality. You do not need his equipment to get this; you need to lift something heavy twice a week and get out of breath a few times. That is free.

Light, food, and the boring wins

Morning light within a short window of waking. A plant-forward diet built on vegetables, legumes, olive oil and whole foods. Moving a little every half hour instead of sitting for four hours straight. These are the rest of the free layer, and they show up in cohort study after cohort study. The fibre target that tracks with 15–30% lower mortality (around 25–29g a day) is a bag of lentils, not a subscription.

So what does the money buy?

Here’s the honest accounting. Johnson has said a “replicable” version of Blueprint runs somewhere around $7,000–$15,000 a year, and that the core habit-based benefits cost under $100 a month — mostly a few well-studied supplements. The rest — the testing, the experimental compounds, the therapies — is the long tail of the 20%, chased at enormous cost for uncertain, unmeasurable-in-one-person gains.

The point isn’t that measurement is worthless — knowing your blood pressure and a few basic numbers genuinely matters, and it’s cheap. The point is that the maximal version is a hobby with a very steep price curve, and the person doing it most publicly keeps telling you the first sliver of spend does almost all the work.
