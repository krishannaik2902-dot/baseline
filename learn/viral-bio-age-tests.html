The viral “biological age” tests are mostly theatre

Journal · 7 July 2026

Late 2025 gave us the knee-to-floor jump test all over TikTok, a fresh wave of “what’s your real age?” quizzes, and a booming market in epigenetic clocks. Some of it is harmless fun. Some of it is being sold to you as science it isn’t. Here’s how to tell which is which — and what to measure instead.

The problem with a single “biological age” number

The premium version of this is the epigenetic clock: a lab reads chemical marks on your DNA and returns an age, often lower than your real one, which feels wonderful. The trouble is what the people who build these clocks say about them. Researchers like Morgan Levine and Matt Kaeberlein have been clear that the clocks were designed to work across populations, not to hand a verdict to one person; that they wobble in response to short-term noise like a recent infection, a diet change, or weight loss; and that they cannot validate what any single intervention did to any single body.

Bryan Johnson’s headline claims — “ageing 0.66 years per year”, “31 years younger” — come from exactly this kind of measurement, and are exactly why scientists push back on them. A number that moves when you catch a cold is not measuring the speed you’re ageing. It’s measuring the cold.

This is why Baseline deliberately refuses to give you a biological-age score. We’d have to invent it, and inventing it would make us the thing we’re warning you about.

The TikTok tests: fun, not diagnostic

The viral movement tests — can you get from standing to sitting on the floor and back without using your hands, can you balance on one leg for ten seconds — are a more interesting case, because they’re gesturing at something real. Strength, balance and mobility genuinely do track with how well people age. A one-legged balance test has shown associations with mortality in older adults.

But a single party-trick rep is not a measurement. It’s influenced by how you slept, whether your knee hurts today, your height, and how much you practised the specific move. Treat these as a nudge to train the underlying quality, not as a readout of your internal age.

What actually tracks how you’re ageing

The honest self-tests are less shareable and more useful. None of them need a lab.

TestWhat it reflectsWhy it’s credible
Grip strengthWhole-body strength and resilienceIn the PURE study (~140,000 people), each 5kg drop in grip was linked to ~16% higher mortality — a stronger signal than blood pressure.
VO₂max (or a hard-effort proxy)Cardiorespiratory fitnessThe least-fit group in a 122,000-person study had ~5× the mortality risk of the fittest — the biggest modifiable gap in the data.
Dead hang / farmer carryGrip endurance and functional strengthRough functional targets exist (a ~2-minute dead hang for men, ~90s for women) and they’re trainable.
Sit-to-stand & single-leg balanceLower-body strength, stabilityBoth track with later-life function and fall risk in cohort studies.

The beauty of these is that the measurement and the intervention are the same thing. Improving your grip means training your grip. You can’t game them, and when the number moves, it moved because you changed something real — which is precisely the property the epigenetic clock lacks.

So what should you do?

Enjoy the jump test if it got you off the sofa. Skip the £300 “ageing speed” panel. And if you want a number to actually chase, pick grip strength or a fitness benchmark and try to beat last quarter’s. That’s the version of this that’s real.
