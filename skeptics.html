What we’re skeptical of

Last updated 7 July 2026

A plan you can trust has to be willing to say “the data isn’t there yet.” Here’s the popular stuff we leave out of your plan, and why. We’d genuinely love some of it to work — the evidence just hasn’t caught up. And since we sell no supplements and take no affiliate money, we’ve no reason to pretend otherwise.

Continuous glucose monitors for people without diabetes

Wearing a CGM when you’re metabolically healthy feels like data-driven insight, but there are no clinical guidelines for interpreting the numbers in non-diabetics — a banana spiking your glucose is normal, not a warning. In practice it tends to produce anxiety and food rules rather than better health. If you have diabetes or pre-diabetes, that’s a different conversation, and one for your doctor.

Our position: Skip it unless a clinician has a specific reason to recommend one.

NMN and resveratrol

These were the anti-ageing supplements of the late 2010s, sold on the idea of boosting NAD+. Human evidence for meaningful longevity or healthspan benefit never really materialised, and even the enthusiasts have quietly moved on. The credible “boring” supplement core — creatine, vitamin D if you’re deficient, omega-3, magnesium — is where the actual evidence sits.

Our position: Not recommended. Interesting biology, absent human payoff.

Hydrogen water

Water with dissolved hydrogen gas, sold at a steep markup with tablets and special bottles. The human trials are small, short, and inconsistent, and the mechanism stories run well ahead of the outcomes.

Our position: Inconclusive at best. Ordinary water, of which most people drink too little, is the move.

Greens powders

Convenient, pleasant, and marketed as an insurance policy against a bad diet — often by people paid handsomely to say so. The evidence that they improve any hard outcome is thin, the doses of individual ingredients are frequently token, and they cost far more than the vegetables they’re standing in for.

Our position: Not worth it for most people. Eat a wider range of actual plants; it’s cheaper and better.

Cold plunges (as sold)

Deliberate cold exposure has real, if modest, effects on mood and alertness — the honest version is in your plan when it fits. What we’re skeptical of is the industry: the $6,000 tubs, the sweeping claims about immunity and metabolism, and the specific weekly targets that trace back to a single researcher’s work (Susanna Søberg’s ~11 minutes a week), not to the person selling the tub. Cold straight after strength training can also blunt the muscle growth you trained for.

Our position: A cold shower is a fine, free version. You don’t need the hardware or the hype.

Red-light therapy panels for longevity

There’s legitimate clinical use of certain light wavelengths for specific skin and wound applications. The leap from there to “buy this panel to live longer” is not supported by good human evidence.

Our position: Limited clinical support for the longevity claims. Not in your plan.

IV drips and vitamin infusions

Unless you have a diagnosed deficiency or absorption problem, an IV bypasses a gut that was working perfectly well and delivers an expensive version of things food and, occasionally, a pill already provide. The “wellness drip” is mostly theatre with a needle.

Our position: Not recommended outside a genuine medical reason.

Rapamycin, metformin, and peptides for healthy people

These come up constantly in longevity circles. As of 2026 there’s no proven anti-ageing benefit in healthy humans — the metformin “TAME” trial hasn’t even run, and a rapamycin trial missed its main endpoint. Grey-market peptides are a regulatory and safety minefield. All of these are prescription or clinician-territory, carry real risks, and have no place in a self-serve plan.

Our position: Never a self-start. If you’re curious, that’s a conversation with a doctor, framed honestly as experimental.

“Biological age” scores

The viral quizzes and epigenetic-clock tests that tell you you’re “really 34” make a compelling headline and a shaky claim. The scientists who build these clocks say plainly that they were designed for study populations, respond to short-term noise like a recent cold or a diet change, and can’t validate what a single person’s intervention did. We deliberately don’t give you an “ageing speed” number, because we’d have to make it up.

Our position: Fun, not real. Your Baseline score measures current habits — the thing you can actually change.

Maximal stacking

The instinct to do everything — sauna and plunge and red light and ten supplements and three tracking devices — is expensive, time-hungry, and low-return. Bryan Johnson, whose Blueprint is the maximal version, says himself that roughly 80% of the results come from free basics done consistently. That’s the whole thesis of this site.

Our position: Pick two or three things that matter and actually do them. Skip the rest without guilt.

This page is educational information, not medical advice. If a clinician has recommended any of the above for you specifically, follow their advice, not a website’s general take.
